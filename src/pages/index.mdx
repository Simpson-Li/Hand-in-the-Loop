## Abstract

<div class="text-justify">
Vision-Language-Action (VLA) models suffers from the accumulation of compounding errors when applied to dexterous manipulation because of high-degree-of-freedom (DoF) action spaces. While Interactive Imitation Learning (IIL) has proven effective for policy refinement through human takeover data, its deployment in dexterous manipulation remains challenging due to ``gesture jumps'' at the moment of intervention. We present Hand-in-the-Loop (HandITL), a seamless interventional method that fuses human correction and policy rollout without bringing gesture jump at takeover moment for bimanual dexterous system. By seamlessly fusing human corrective intent with the model's ongoing actions, HandITL reduces the intervention jitter by 99.8\% and preserves robust manipulation capabilities post-takeover with improving mean finish time by 19.1\% and reducing grasp failures by 87.5\%, compared to teleoperation. Our method is designed to support complex bimanual coordination, tool use, and fine-grained long-horizon tasks. Extensive real-world experiments on dexterous VLA models demonstrate that HandITL significantly outperforms the policy trained with standard teleoperation data by 19\% on average on three long-horizon dexterous tasks, effectively mitigating compounding errors and substantially boosting task scores.
</div>

## Method

### Intervention Modes

<div class="text-justify">
During standard autonomous execution, the robot executes the first action of the predicted sequence <LaTeX formula="\mathbf{A}_t^{\pi}" inline />. In our human-in-the-loop setting, the operator can inject a corrective action <LaTeX formula="\mathbf{a}_{t}^{h}" inline /> during the rollout. The final executed command <LaTeX formula="\mathbf{a}_{t}^{exec}" inline /> is a blended output:<LaTeX formula="\mathbf{a}_{t}^{exec} = \alpha \mathbf{a}_{t}^{\pi} + \beta \mathbf{a}_{t}^{h}" inline />, where <LaTeX formula="\alpha \in [0, 1]" inline /> denotes the policy weight and <LaTeX formula="\beta \in [0, 1]" inline /> denotes the human authority weight. The details of fusing arm and hand commands are shown in the method illustration. Our method supports two primary intervention modes:


• Full Takeover: <LaTeX formula="\alpha \approx 0, \beta = 1" inline />. The robot is primarily controlled by the human, which is crucial for recovering from severe OOD states.

• Copilot Shared Control: The operator injects minimal corrective signals while preserving the base policy's intent. The system allows decoupled authority weights for the arms <LaTeX formula="\beta_a" inline /> and hands <LaTeX formula="\beta_h = 1 - \alpha_h" inline />.

</div>

### Optimization-Based Relative Hand Retargeting
<div class="text-justify">
Traditional absolute retargeting maps human poses directly to the robot, causing sudden ``gesture jumps'' during interventions that destabilize contact-rich grasps. To eliminate this, we propose an optimization-based relative retargeting algorithm. At each control step <LaTeX formula="t" inline />, we solve for a target hand configuration <LaTeX formula="q_t" inline /> that tracks the human's relative 、keyvectors while enforcing safety constraints. To ensure robustness against tracking outliers, we employ the Huber loss <LaTeX formula="\mathcal{H}_\delta(\cdot)" inline />. The optimization objective <LaTeX formula="\mathcal{J}(q_t)" inline /> is formulated as:

<LaTeX formula="\begin{aligned} \min_{q_t} \mathcal{J}(q_t) = & \underbrace{\sum_{i=1}^{5} \mathcal{H}_\delta \Big( \beta_i(d_i) \big( \Delta \mathbf{v}_{i}^{\text{rob}}(q_t) - \Delta \hat{\mathbf{v}}_{i}^{\text{hum}} \big) \Big)}_{\mathcal{L}_{\text{shape}} \text{ (Global Shaping)}} + \underbrace{\sum_{j \in \mathcal{F}_{\text{opp}}} \mathcal{H}_\delta \Big( \omega_j(d_j) \big( \mathbf{u}_{j}^{\text{rob}}(q_t) - \alpha_j(d_j) \hat{\mathbf{u}}_{j}^{\text{tgt}} \big) \Big)}_{\mathcal{L}_{\text{grasp}} \text{ (Precision Grasping)}} \\ & + \underbrace{\sum_{(m,n) \in \mathcal{C}} \gamma \max \left(0, \frac{d_{\text{safe}}}{D_{mn}(q_t)} - 1 \right)}_{\mathcal{L}_{\text{safe}} \text{ (Structural Safety)}} + \underbrace{\mathcal{H}_\delta \Big( \lambda_{\text{reg}} (q_t - q_{t-1}) \Big)}_{\mathcal{L}_{\text{reg}} \text{ (Temporal Regularization)}}, \end{aligned}" />

where superscripts <LaTeX formula="\text{rob}" inline /> and <LaTeX formula="\text{hum}" inline /> denote robot and human states, and <LaTeX formula="\Delta (\cdot) = (\cdot)_t - (\cdot)_{t_0}" inline /> represents the relative motion since the intervention timestamp <LaTeX formula="t_0" inline />. The loss components are defined as follows:

<ul class="w-full max-w-none self-stretch list-outside list-disc pl-8 space-y-2 text-left">
  <li><strong>Global Shaping (<LaTeX formula="\mathcal{L}_{\text{shape}}" inline />):</strong> Prioritizes overall posture via wrist-to-fingertip keyvectors <LaTeX formula="\mathbf{v}_i" inline />. Here, <LaTeX formula="\Delta \mathbf{v}_{i}^{\text{rob}}(q_t)" inline /> and <LaTeX formula="\Delta \hat{\mathbf{v}}_{i}^{\text{hum}}" inline /> are the relative changes of these vectors on the robot and human hands. The adaptive weight <LaTeX formula="\beta_i(d_i)" inline /> decays via a sigmoid as the absolute human thumb-to-finger distance <LaTeX formula="d_i" inline /> decreases, dominating when the hand is open.</li>
  <li><strong>Precision Grasping (<LaTeX formula="\mathcal{L}_{\text{grasp}}" inline />):</strong> Captures pinch grasping via thumb-to-fingertip keyvectors <LaTeX formula="\mathbf{u}_j" inline />. The robot's absolute keyvector <LaTeX formula="\mathbf{u}_{j}^{\text{rob}}(q_t)" inline /> tracks the nominal target <LaTeX formula="\hat{\mathbf{u}}_{j}^{\text{tgt}} = \mathbf{u}_{j}^{\text{rob}}(t_0) + \Delta \hat{\mathbf{u}}_{j}^{\text{hum}}" inline />. We introduce a pinch activation factor <LaTeX formula="\alpha_j(d_j) \in [0,1]" inline />. When <LaTeX formula="d_j" inline /> falls below a threshold, <LaTeX formula="\alpha_j(d_j) \to 0" inline /> explicitly drives <LaTeX formula="\mathbf{u}_{j}^{\text{rob}}" inline /> to <LaTeX formula="\mathbf{0}" inline /> (i.e., firm contact). Concurrently, the weight <LaTeX formula="\omega_j(d_j) \propto (1 - \alpha_j)" inline /> sharply increases, ensuring this term dominates during closed grasps.</li>
  <li><strong>Structural Safety (<LaTeX formula="\mathcal{L}_{\text{safe}}" inline />):</strong> A repulsive barrier preventing self-collisions. <LaTeX formula="D_{mn}(q_t)" inline /> is the absolute physical distance between adjacent PIP/DIP joint pairs <LaTeX formula="(m,n) \in \mathcal{C}" inline />. The penalty <LaTeX formula="\gamma" inline /> activates only when <LaTeX formula="D_{mn}" inline /> falls below the safety margin <LaTeX formula="d_{\text{safe}}" inline />.</li>
  <li><strong>Temporal Regularization (<LaTeX formula="\mathcal{L}_{\text{reg}}" inline />):</strong> Penalizes the joint displacement <LaTeX formula="(q_t - q_{t-1})" inline />, scaled by <LaTeX formula="\lambda_{\text{reg}}" inline />, to prevent high-frequency jitter.</li>
</ul>

</div>

### Velocity-Based Shard Arm Control

<div class="text-justify">
Since VR controllers are rigidly mounted on the gloves, operators lack access to physical joysticks. To enable intuitive corrections, we design a velocity-based shared-control interface. Human wrist motions are translated into residual twists, smoothed via Exponential Moving Average (EMA), and injected into the policy trunk without persistent drift.

At step <LaTeX formula="t" inline />, the VLA outputs the target pose and feedforward twist <LaTeX formula="(\mathbf{p}_t^{\pi}, \mathbf{R}_t^{\pi}, \dot{\mathbf{p}}_t^{\pi}, \boldsymbol{\omega}_t^{\pi})" inline />. Simultaneously, the residual human twist <LaTeX formula="(\dot{\mathbf{p}}_t^{h}, \boldsymbol{\omega}_t^{h})" inline /> is estimated over a window of <LaTeX formula="k=2" inline /> VR ticks (<LaTeX formula="\Delta T = 0.04\,\mathrm{s}" inline />) to balance stability and latency:

<LaTeX formula="\begin{aligned} \dot{\mathbf{p}}_{t}^{h} &= \text{EMA}\left(\frac{\mathbf{p}_t^{vr} - \mathbf{p}_{t-k}^{vr}}{\Delta T}\right), \quad  \boldsymbol{\omega}_{t}^{h} = \text{EMA}\left(\frac{\log\left((\mathbf{R}_{t-k}^{vr})^{-1}\mathbf{R}_t^{vr}\right)}{\Delta T}\right). \end{aligned}" />

These twists are integrated into incremental pose offsets <LaTeX formula="(\Delta \mathbf{p}_t^{h}, \Delta \mathbf{R}_t^{h})" inline /> to compute the final corrective target pose:

<LaTeX formula="\mathbf{p}_t^{\text{tgt}} = \mathbf{p}_t^{\pi} + \Delta \mathbf{p}_t^{h}, \quad \mathbf{R}_t^{\text{tgt}} = \mathbf{R}_t^{\pi} \Delta \mathbf{R}_t^{h}" />

A task-space PD tracker, augmented with policy feedforward terms, then computes the commanded spatial velocity.

Crucially, deriving residuals from relative motion ensures that when the operator is still, <LaTeX formula="(\dot{\mathbf{p}}_t^{h}, \boldsymbol{\omega}_t^{h}) \rightarrow \mathbf{0}" inline />. The EMA filter smoothly decays these residuals, eliminating persistent offsets without requiring the operator to manually return to a neutral ``center'' position.

</div>

## Experiments

<div class="text-justify">
Our experiments consist of three parts: (1) takeover command discontinuity analysis, which measures the abrupt change in dexterous hand commands at the handover moment; (2) post-takeover manipulation evaluation, which tests whether each method preserves sufficient dexterous manipulation capability after takeover; and (3) long-horizon policy evaluation, which examines whether the collected human-correction data improves the performance of a VLA policy on challenging bimanual dexterous manipulation tasks.
</div>

### Takeover Command Discontinuity Analysis
<div class="text-justify">
We evaluate takeover command discontinuity on the Drill and Bread Clip tasks.Operators first establish a stable teleoperation on the tool, switch to rollout mode, and then toggle the takeover mode three times per trial. 

<Figure slot="right">
  <Image slot="figure" source={jitter} altText="data_pyramid" class="w-[80%]" />
  <span slot="caption">Takeover command discontinuity on the Drill (top) and Bread Clip (bottom) tasks.</span>
</Figure>

Across 30 attempts per task-method pair, direct teleoperation switching exhibited a high failure rate, frequently dropping the tool or releasing the drill trigger due to abrupt gesture jumps. In contrast, our relative retargeting method seamlessly preserved grasp stability.

Our method drastically reduces quantitative command mutation at intervention. On the Bread Clip task, which requires an open hand posture that maximizes human-robot state mismatch, direct switching introduces massive command spikes (mean <LaTeX formula="\approx 4.38 \times 10^{-2}" inline />). Our method suppresses this to <LaTeX formula="\approx 6.8 \times 10^{-5}" inline />, reducing the discontinuity by <strong>99.8%</strong> and outperforming the DeltaCmd baseline (<LaTeX formula="\approx 6.23 \times 10^{-3}" inline />) by nearly two orders of magnitude. Conversely, the Drill task involves a closed power grasp, naturally restricting the maximum pose mismatch and yielding numerically smaller jumps for direct switching (<LaTeX formula="\approx 2.75 \times 10^{-2}" inline />). However, even this seemingly minor mutation is catastrophic, instantly causing the index finger to slip off the trigger. Our method resolves this by achieving a near-zero mean command change (<LaTeX formula="\approx 2.65 \times 10^{-4}" inline />), comparable to the Jacobian baseline but without its inherent singularity issues, ensuring stable trigger actuation and functional tool use.
</div>

### Post-Takeover Manipulation Capability

<div class="text-justify">
We evaluate pure manipulation capability during takeover on the Pick Up and Place the Parts and Pick Up the Drill task. Operators initiated both tasks directly in an active takeover state. If an operator reached their physical workspace limit, they could temporarily disengage, reset their hand posture, and re-engage (recorded as Interventions).

  <span slot="caption">Post-takeover manipulation capability on the Pick Up and Place the Parts and Pick Up the Drill tasks. </span>
</Figure>

<Figure>
  <Image slot="figure" source={failure_mode} altText="makeup_result" class="w-[80%]" />

As shown in the table, on the Pick Up and Place the Parts task, our method achieves the fastest mean time (<LaTeX formula="42.8\,\mathrm{s}" inline />), improving by <LaTeX formula="19.1\%" inline />, and reduces grasp failures (retries) by <LaTeX formula="87.5\%" inline /> compared to Teleop. Furthermore, our method exhibits minimal cross-operator variance (a <LaTeX formula="5.6\,\mathrm{s}" inline /> gap vs. <LaTeX formula="18.0\,\mathrm{s}" inline /> for Teleop), proving its high intuitiveness. As illustrated in the failure analysis, this performance gap stems from the underlying kinematics. Both our Relative method and Teleop utilize optimization algorithms, ensuring accurate thumb-to-finger opposition and collision avoidance. Conversely, the Jacobian and Delta Cmd baselines rely on previous-step differential calculations. They struggle with precise pinching and easily accumulate drift during fast motions, resulting in awkward grasping postures, frequent part drops, and significantly longer completion times (<LaTeX formula="68.0\,\mathrm{s}" inline /> and <LaTeX formula="56.7\,\mathrm{s}" inline />, respectively).
On the Pick Up the Drill task, our method again demonstrates better overall efficiency. It records the lowest mean completion time (<LaTeX formula="14.4\,\mathrm{s}" inline />), the fewest workspace interventions (<LaTeX formula="11/10" inline />), and maintains better cross-operator time consistency (<LaTeX formula="11.0\,\mathrm{s}" inline /> / <LaTeX formula="17.9\,\mathrm{s}" inline />) compared to the differential baselines. Because our relative retargeting employs a different optimization formulation than the teleoperation, the mapping of fine finger articulation differs slightly. Consequently, without tactile feedback, operators require a brief period of adaptation and postural adjustment to command, which slightly affect the success rate of triggering the button.

### Long-Horizon Policy Evaluation
<div class="text-justify">
</div>

<Figure>
  <Image slot="figure" source={experiment} altText="makeup_result" class="w-full" />
  <span slot="caption">Execution sequences of the three long-horizon bimanual dexterous manipulation tasks.</span>
</Figure>

<div class="text-justify">
We collect a 20-hour real-world teleoperation dataset, evenly distributed across three long-horizon tasks. 
Starting from a pre-trained GR-Dexter policy, we further fine-tune the model on this teleoperation dataset to obtain our base policy.
We then deploy the base policy on real robots and collect takeover data during rollouts whenever the operator judges intervention to be necessary. 
We collect correction data under both full-takeover and copilot shared-control modes in an HG-DAgger-style takeover process. 
For post-training, we compare the following policies:

<ul class="w-full max-w-none self-stretch list-outside list-disc pl-8 space-y-2 text-left">
  <li><strong>Base</strong>: the policy fine-tuned only on the 20-hour teleoperation dataset (base dataset);</li>
  <li><strong>Teleop_old</strong>: continuing to fine-tune the base policy using the base dataset only;</li>
  <li><strong>Teleop_new</strong>: fine-tuning the base policy using a 0.5:1 mixture of 1-hour new teleoperation data and base dataset;</li>
  <li><strong>Copilot</strong>: fine-tuning the base policy using a 0.5:1 mixture of copilot takeover data and base dataset;</li>
  <li><strong>Full Takeover</strong>: fine-tuning the base policy using a 0.5:1 mixture of full-takeover data and base dataset.</li>
</ul>

For a fair comparison, all additional post-training datasets, including new teleoperation, copilot takeover, and full-takeover data, are 1 hour in duration, and all post-training runs use identical training schedules and hyperparameters.

We evaluate all policies on three long-horizon tasks requiring high-precision bimanual coordination. Each task is decomposed into several semantic sub-stages. We report the average Sub-goal Completion Score over 10 evaluation rollouts for each model.

<Figure>
  <Image slot="figure" source={evaluation} altText="long_horizon" class="w-full" />
  <span slot="caption">Average normalized sub-goal completion scores across three long-horizon tasks.</span>
</Figure>

Comparing the five policies reveals three critical insights regarding long-horizon performance:

<ol class="w-full max-w-none self-stretch list-outside list-decimal pl-8 space-y-3 text-left">
  <li><strong>Limited Improvements from Pure Teleoperation:</strong> Simply increasing pure teleoperation data brings limited and inconsistent gains. Both Teleop_old and Teleop_new show only marginal improvements, and their effects vary across tasks. This suggests that additional off-policy demonstrations do not sufficiently address the rollout states where compounding errors occur, especially in late phases that require precise contact-rich manipulation.</li>
  <li><strong>Effectiveness of Intervention Data:</strong> In contrast, policies fine-tuned with intervention data achieve higher average normalized completion scores. Since Copilot and Full Takeover data are collected when the deployed policy actually requires human correction, they provide targeted supervision for recovering from out-of-distribution states. This makes intervention data more effective than standard teleoperation demonstrations for improving long-horizon robustness.</li>
  <li><strong>Copilot vs. Full Takeover:</strong> Among the two intervention strategies, Copilot generally yields the strongest overall performance. Unlike Full Takeover, which may introduce larger action deviations during authority switching, Copilot preserves the base policy's ongoing behavior while injecting local corrections. The resulting data stays closer to the policy's rollout distribution, leading to more stable downstream improvements.</li>
</ol>
